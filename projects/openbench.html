Rube Goldberg Camp and Membership

Open Bench Project | Portland, ME

Member and Camp Counselor, Summer 2016

In the summer of 2016, I volunteered at a week long Rube Goldberg camp for kids ages 10 to 13 at a maker space in Portland, ME, called Open Bench Project. In looking for activities to get involved in during my summer at home, I found Open Bench on the internet and went to go visit the space as it is a five minute drive from my house. Here is where I met the founder and CEO of the space, who had turned an abandoned wharehouse into a community maker space. We decided on a deal: a free membership to use the space in exchange for help as a camp counselor at the Open Benchâ€™s annual Rube Goldberg camp.


The Open Bench Project is a startup community makerspace in Portland, Maine. Members pay a monthly fee to use the 7,500 square foot facility equipped with a plethora of tools and machines. Here I had my first experience welding, using both wood and metal lathes, and 3D plotting. I was also able to further my skills in 3D printing and woodworking.


In July, I volunteered at a Summer camp where eleven middle schoolers came to learn about simple machines and ultimately build a Rube Goldberg Apparatus. Campers experienced challenges in incorporating multiple simple machines into a functioning labyrinth of their own. I assisted in helping them think through each process, and the effects each type of machine would have on their apparatus. The campers also learned the many steps to the design cycle. They were required to show a proof of concept before moving on to the construction of their apparatus. It was my job to not only teach them about the steps of the design cycle, but also help them in many of the manufacturing skills that they were unable to perform. This included using table, band, and hole saws, 3D printers, and lathes to fabricate the mechanisms in their Rube Goldberg machines.


By the end of the week, we successfully connected the eleven individual apparatuses into one massive Rube Goldberg machine. A few examples of the components in this final product included the launching of a rocket engine, a Coke & Mentos explosion, a homemade confetti gun, pouring acetone to melt styrofoam, and finally a watermelon falling from a 30 foot ceiling only to smash into the ground. And these of course were all connected through simple machines. This experience enhanced my competence in a machine shop, taught me important lessons in how to effectively harness motivation and curiosity in young kids, and finally deepened my passion for mechanical design and community spaces for creation.
