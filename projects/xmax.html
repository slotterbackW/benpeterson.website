The XMAX Project

Enabling Engineering | Northeastern University


Mechanical Engineering Team Member, Spring 2016 - Present

“Enabling Engineering is a student organization that designs and builds low-cost devices to improve the lives of older adults, individuals with physical and cognitive disabilities, and the caregivers who assist them. Our projects aim to empower affected individuals by giving them greater independence, reducing medical burdens, and increasing social connectedness.” Personally, I have been working on the XMAX Project since the winter of 2016. The purpose of this project is to create a re-engineered version of an XBOX controller that enables those with limited motion ability in their limbs to play XBOX. Check out the inspiration for the project and how far we’ve come below!


Project Information

The inspiration for this project came from one of my friends, Dylan, who was the manager for the varsity men’s basketball team here at Northeastern. He would attend practices and games, and would hang out with another one of the team’s active attendants: Max. Max is an 18-year-old who loves sports and sits on the bench with the team at their home games. Max also has cerebral palsy and has extremely limited mobility in his arms and legs. This does not hold Max back in the slightest; however, there are many things that Max would love to do but is not able to due to his condition. One of those is playing XBOX. Dylan, a fellow mechanical engineering student and involved in the Enabling Engineering project, realized the potential to make this wish a reality.

In order to make an XBOX control compatible with Max’s abilities, we had to learn about where he was strongest. Through speaking with his father and physical therapist, we learned that Max had the most mobility in his head, and his neck was by fair his strongest muscle. He is also in the active process of improving the ability to actuate his hands and shoulders, which he is practicing with his therapist. With this information, we designed a system that would allow the XBOX’s major buttons (A, B, X, and Y) to be evenly spaced around his head. We also enlarged the analog sticks to golfball-sized controls that Max could hold onto with his entire hand.

In the spring of 2017 we brought our first prototype to Max’s physical therapy facility to conduct user-testing. This provided us with important insights on how Max interacted with the device, information from his physical therapist about ways to improve the design, and also the opportunity to see Max throw a touchdown pass in Madden for the first time in his life. We took all of this learning back to campus and have been working on the second generation of the XMAX controller, which is almost complete. This version, shown in the pictures below, has a more robust aluminum frame, and will include an additional conventional controller that accompanies the system, so that someone can help Max if he gets stuck.

This project has allowed me to bring many of the product development skills I learned on coop to campus and work with a team of students to achieve a common goal. From a technical engineering side, I’ve gained experience with PCB and arduino integration, soldering and housing electronics, and human factors engineering in designing a product that would suit Max’s needs. Additionally, I’ve found a project that directly improves the life of an individual, making the work much easier to do and my motivation to complete it ever higher. Not only will this device enable Max to play XBOX, but it can also be seen (especially by his therapist) as a tool to help Max gain more mobility, strengthen his muscles, and have fun while doing it! We are hoping to deliver our second generation prototype to Max by the end of October, 2017. Check in on the project here.
