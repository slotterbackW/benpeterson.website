The XMAX Project

The inspiration for this project came from one of my friends, Dylan, who was the manager for the varsity men’s basketball team here at Northeastern. He would attend practices and games, and would hang out with another one of the team’s active attendants: Max. Max is an 18-year-old who loves sports and sits on the bench with the team at their home games. Max also has cerebral palsy and has extremely limited mobility in his arms and legs. This does not hold Max back in the slightest; however, there are many things that Max would love to do but is not able to due to his condition. One of those is playing XBOX. Dylan, a fellow mechanical engineering student and involved in the Enabling Engineering project, realized the potential to make this wish a reality. The XMAX Project was launched with the goal of creating a solution designed for Max to play XBOX.

  • Our first step was to learn about Max’s strengths, his mobility, and his expectations for this device. Through interviews with his father and physical therapist, we found that Max had the most mobility in his head, and his neck was by far his strongest muscle.
  • By transforming the information collected in interviews into constraints, we developed a prototype designed around Max’s strengths. Four buttons were to sit around Max’s head, and enlarged analog sticks were fixed to the arms of his chair such that he could use his entire shoulder instead of articulating his thumbs.
  • The first generation of the XMAX controller was completed in the spring of 2017. This consisted of a PVC frame that hooked to the back of Max’s wheelchair. Using GoPro mounts, mechanical buttons were assembled to sit around Max’s head, simulating the A, X, B, and Y buttons on an XBOX controller.
  • We delivered the controller to Max and conducted user testing alongside his physical therapist. The concept solution showed significant promise, and Max threw his first touchdown pass. We also came away with key insights that would drive the next iteration of the device.
  • The second generation XMAX controller was completed in the spring of 2018. It included a more robust aluminum frame, modular buttons with flexible linkages for adjusting their position around Max’s head, and a companion controller so that another person could help Max play if he got stuck.
  • This improvement involved hard wiring the A, X, B, and Y buttons on the XBOX controller to an assistive “breakout” controller. Connector pins were used to wiring this “breakout” controller to the buttons on the XMAX device. Doing this allowed for another person to have full functionality of the XBOX controller to help Max if he needed.
  • This decision was made after significant testing with Max. It was clear that while enabling Max to play XBOX was the ultimate goal, this would need to be taken in stages. The enlarged analog sticks on the first prototype did not provide the easy articulation we had hoped. We decided it was equally important to prioritize XMAX’s functionality and Max’s engagement. Therefore, by simplifying the XMAX device to the four main buttons, we simplified Max’s responsibilities while still allowing him to be in control of the game.
  • This project has allowed me to bring many of the product development skills I learned on coop to campus and work with a team of students to achieve a common goal. From a technical engineering side, I’ve gained experience with PCB and arduino integration, soldering and housing electronics, and human factors engineering in designing a product that would suit Max’s needs. Additionally, I’ve found a project that directly improves the life of an individual, coupling my passions for engineering design and social change. This process has taught me that these are the types of projects I hope to be involved in throughout my career.
  • Not only will this device enable Max to play XBOX, but it can also be seen (especially by his therapist) as a tool to help Max gain more mobility, strengthen his muscles, and have fun while doing it. Click on the video below to see Max throw one of his first touchdowns!
