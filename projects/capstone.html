Background

Northeastern’s mechanical engineering curriculum finishes with Senior Capstone Design Project. On behalf of Lyndra Therapeutics, myself and four other senior engineering students developed a first of its kind artificial stomach which simulates the mechanical and chemical environment inside the human stomach. We were awarded the “Extra Mile Award” for delivering a complete and functional system to Lyndra after the six-month capstone term. A provisional patent has been filed for the stomach device.

Lyndra’s problem statement was to design an anatomically representative environment capable of simulating the digestive forces in a human stomach. This required solutions for creating a dimensionally accurate stomach (the stomach bag), simulating the motion of the pyloric sphincter and peristaltic waves, and control systems for temperature and fluid flow. These four different modules can be seen highlighted in the final CAD of the system below.

  • Understanding that this was an ambitious scope to accomplish in a six-month development cycle, the team recognized that organization and project management would be key. While each team member contributed to all aspects of the system, each individual was responsible to lead the development of a single module. I was responsible for the peristaltic motion design so I will focus on this module in greatest detail. A photo of the final product of this module assembled around the stomach bag can be seen on the left.
  • Initial brainstorms helped to breakdown peristaltic motion into a mechanical design challenge with the creation of design goals and constraints. The final design must:
    • Achieve longitudinal and radial motion to constrict around and propagate down the stomach bag simultaneously.
    • Constrict concentrically around the stomach bag to achieve uniform pressure and accurately mimic the physiological process.
    • Achieve an occlusion range from 0-100%, meaning the ability to decrease the cross-sectional diameter of the stomach bag to zero.
    • Limit the amount of pinch points to avoid tearing the stomach bag.
    • Propagate down and constrict around the stomach at physiologically representative speeds.
  • Initial sketches of peristaltic wave constriction (top row) and propagation (bottom row) can be seen on the right.
  • By conducting a decision matrix analysis the “Bearing Snake Mechanism” was deemed the superior design concept.
  • This design involves two identical pin-in-slot mechanisms that sit on either side of the stomach bag. The paths of the curved slots are designed specifically to facilitate concentric constriction around the stomach bag. The two linkages of the mechanism are attached to either end of a “bearing snake.”
  • The bearing snake feature is comprised of an elastic spring-steel core that strings together 25 radial bearings. The bearings roll down the stomach bag decreasing friction in the system and the elastic core allows for a variable radius to simulate a peristaltic wave. The first prototype of the mechanism is seen on the left.
  • A linear test fixture and straight-tube silicone stomach bag were created so that we could test the Bearing Snake Mechanism at low fidelity before proceeding.
  • The video to the left shows how the bearing snake is able to simultaneously constrict around the stomach bag while propagating up and down with limited frictional resistance.
  • This first iteration of the mechanism showed great promise. The major areas of improvement were reducing the amount of friction between and the pin and slot, increasing rigidity in the system, and incorporating a solution for automated motion.
  • The second iteration of the mechanism aimed to address the improvements highlighted above. Notable changes involved:
    • Including a top plate with the same pin-in-slot configuration to sandwich the linkages in place and increase the rigidity of the system.
    • Adding radial bearings to the pins such that the interaction between pin and slot was now rolling contact instead of sliding contact, significantly reducing friction.
    • Adding two pulleys (shown in yellow in the exploded-view CAD image shown left). One connects the linkages to a linear actuator while the other connects to a spring-return that always keeps the system in tension.
  • The final version of the peristaltic wave mechanism was assembled and its accuracy was validated using the original kinematic study sketches from SolidWorks that drove the design of the curved slots. As shown in the video above, the center of the bearing snake maintains concentricity with the center of the kinematic sketch throughout constriction. Using a motion-tracking software, the system was validated to hold concentricity within +/- 0.080”.
  • The final version of the bearing snake and machined linkages can be seen to the right.

After just 6-months of development, we were able to provide Lyndra with a complete and operational system that will allow them to conduct comprehensive in-vitro testing for the first time. The system is able to continuously cycle through multiple digestive cycles and simulate physiologically representative gastric motility. The system was delivered to Lyndra’s offices and a provisional patent has been filed for the device. A video of device’s peristaltic motion can be seen below.
