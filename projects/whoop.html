Mini Buckle Stiffening Project

  • The “mini buckle” is the area that allows the strap to connect to the band. It is composed of two crossbars on the rear end of the injection molded bottom housing.
  • Broken mini buckles are the leading mechanical defect of the WHOOP 2.0, responsible for over 25% of all customer returns.
  • Mini buckles break because they are exposed to large tensile forces when the band is closed tightly around a users wrist. Often the plastic design and thin crossbars that hold the band in place are unable to withstand these forces and resulting in a crack (shown left).
  • I was assigned to come up with a steel-safe solution to reduce the percentage of mechanical failures in the mini buckle in the WHOOP 2.0.
  • My solution involved adding material to the mini buckle in ways that would increase structural rigidity but not significantly change the industrial design (shown right).
  • I designed two configurations of mini buckle updates and conducted analysis on each using SolidWorks’ Finite Element Analysis tool. Shown left, all three configurations have been superimposed on each other to highlight the increased cross-sectional area.
  • The greatest amount of material was added to the corner area of the mini buckle shown in these cross-sections as this is where the greatest stress concentrations are observed. By increasing the radius of curvature between the crossbar and the sidewall, resulting stresses due to axial loading were decreased.
  • FEA was performed by applying 40 lbs of force in the direction shown right to simulate the band pulling on the mini buckle as it sits tightly around the wrist. The rest of the lower housing part was fixed, resulting in max stress and deformation values strictly in the crossbars of the mini buckle.
  • The results (seen left) yielded a 24.6% decrease in maximum deformation and a 40% decrease in maximum stress between the stronger mini buckle and the current version. These values were further improved in the strongest mini buckle configuration (most material added), showing 32.3% and 43.7% decreases in deformation and stress respectively.
  • An experimental study was also conducted to validate analytical results. Each mini buckle configuration (including the current version) was 3D printed such that it would sit orthogonal to the ground as seen in the photo on the left.
  • Using a shortened band and a Mark-10 Force Gauge, the mini buckle prints were loaded to failure. The resulting maximum axial force applied was recorded. The results of this study confirmed the results from the FEA analysis.
  • The team ended up selecting the “stronger” mini buckle option shown in purple in the CAD images above. Before finishing my coop I lead the DFM process with our manufacturer to implement the tooling modifications into production.
  • During my 6-months at WHOOP, I was able to contribute to much more than the strengthening of the mini buckle. I lead the initial design and prototyping of the next generation product to mitigate risks in early stage development. Additionally, I designed two parts in the assembly of the WHOOP 2.0 to increase safety and reliability and was trusted to implement the new assembly process on the production line with our tier 1 manufacturer in California.
